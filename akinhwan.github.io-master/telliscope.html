Duration: 2 months Tags: Branding, UX Design

Telliscope LLC is a startup within the 1776 Incubator, founded by Mellena Haile and Jon Hersh. They offer a platform for entrepreneurs, organizations, and investors to stay up to date on happenings in their community and to form fruitful partnerships with one another.



The Problem

From the outset this was a unique problem because of its relative infancy in development and previous baggage it brought to the table. There were previous designs done in a variety of forms, but mainly in word documents, and in Axure. Such a starting point may have been more of a con than a pro but it framed the initial conversation and gave a precedent to immediately start comparing early ideas with. Visual design aside, what I viewed as the main problem was consistency across the page layouts, and a lack of clarity in arriving at what a given page was for. The feeling was that there wasn't enough activity reflecting the social component of the platform, and the look was unimaginative and dull. Some things could be addressed with updated and modern patterns for basic navigation and layout design, but the more subtle gut reactions one got from using the platform were to be remedied along the process.

Strategy & Goal       ↑

From the design perspective, almost anything could atleast be imagined, however knowing the result of the design may be bigger than one could chew, only the key features were to be fleshed out for this newest prototype. The end result of this project was to be in a fully interactive, responsive, and functional prototype that felt and looked like the real thing as far as the unsuspecting viewer knew but didn't have any backend support.

The way that Telliscope was pitched to me had to be re-pitched back to the client in a way that prioritized the user ecosystem. Clearly every feature of the platform could be distilled into how and why the Entrepreneur, Resource Partner, or Investor might use the site.

The goal was to take the ideas that were hinted at in previous versions and take them a bit further into fully formed ideas. Ofcourse making everything pixel perfect and giving it a professional polish was expected of any product in the current marketplace, Africa or otherwise. We would also take ideas that were completely missing from the original and introduce them, and justify why there was a need for them in the new version. Finally, we would find ideas that could be realized for the new version that are not going to be designed anyways, but are still important to expand the scope to what can't be done either for future reference or as a barometer for realized concepts.

Research       ↑

We began with a company debrief in person at a conference room on the 10th floor of the Vornado building in Crystal City. I took copious notes as to the aspirations of the company that Mellena had in mind. It was clear to me that there was a tremendous amount of thought and care that had already been invested in the project and proceeded with caution. From her own marketing research, there was a vacancy in the field of business networking solutions in Eastern Sub-Saharan Africa. Many entrepreneurs were coming up with innovative and progressive ideas to revolutionize their respective fledgling economies, but were invisible to the world wide investor pool. On the other side of the token, many investors we spoke with were interested in investing in these communities, but were far-away and hesitant without a proper introduction to some of the grassroots movements. The main concern on the Entrepreneur side was Awareness and Opportunity, and for Investors was Risk and Misinformation. There had to be created a window between these parties to ensure at least a chance to exist for furthering relations and realizing dreams.



Some of the insights are not necessarily epiphanous per say for the client, but are articulated in such a way as to resonate and inform further decisions that were not the norm before. This small difference in the mindset can impact the strategic long term plan for the company and product for the better. There may be a constantly revolving door of issues and logistics for the founder, but knowing who will eventually be utilizing the product is critical and can seem very abstract and far away in the early stages.

Ideation       ↑

Stemming from some inspiration I goaded from the client, I began creating some logo designs to kickstart the rest of the prototype. The Fibonacci sequence was referred to, as well as the letter T and circular forms. So I began with logos with rotational symmetry, and created some further iterations with a blue color that would become the main theme. Some reactions to the far southeastern logo was that it was too engineering like, or resembled a thunderstrike. Certainly not what I had intended, so we iterated for the 7th time and it worked like a charm. Eventually we settled on an actual telliscope encircled representing a capital T and the actual object itself. People's first impressions of the final logo was clear, direct, and obvious. All things that worked for the brand, and had the added benefit of being recognizable in other marketing material formats.

Iteration       ↑

We decided upon a bi-weekly video chat conference to go over new additions, and changes to the site. After converting most of the original ideas into a visible and workable prototype, the real work began. Most of the iterations were made possible by directly commenting and marking up the prototype via the UX-Pin Commenting tool. This allowed for immediate re-working of the prototype. Most instances where it was a matter of placement or styling, it was a quick fix. However having the prototype available everywhere and at anytime, allowed for more deep-rooted problems to arise when users tested it from their respective devices. Bugs and errors aside from the UX-Pin platform, I worked to compensate for all the differences in what people were seeing.

  • Copywriting was either too grandiose or obscured by the fact that it was in the wrong place at the wrong time.
  • Users were unable to identify where to input their information
  • Documents were normally unprepared, and therefore had to be written on the spot
  • Events and Announcements were too similar in appearance to distinguish their different purposes
  • The messages were not diverse enough in content, and were un-editable for personalized conversations

Finalization       ↑

As the desktop version was finalized, I then went into re-sizing the prototype accounting for three other screen resolutions (namely mobile, tablet, and laptop). Although there are automatically responsive development methods, I had to manually visualize the breakpoints at which the screens would adapt to changing frames. In most cases, the card layout was highly adaptable for the gradually narrowing screens, and where there were particularly wide and horizontal features, they were broken down into pieces or excluded particular elements in order to simplify the layouts. For form input fields, there would be a zoom effect so as to better see what one was typing. If there were grids or tables that were an odd number, then we justified the elements instead of leaving gaping holes. The design was well received by many of the early testees, and were surprised by the evolution of the design from the previous versions that were presented to them. The overall consensus was that it was sleeker, information was easier to find and recollect, and the site seemed more alive and realistic. The interactions were received better than I thought, and were intuitive enough to be used without any explanation beforehand. Expanding tabs and hover treatments were welcomed as part of the user's learned vocabulary. Among the things I wish I could have improved were the speed and turnover rate, the number of user tests, and developer feedback. Given that this was a scrappy startup team, I wore many hats and created many visual artifacts, this may have detracted from more strategic design thinking. Working with developers and data scientists from the start would have been beneficial, and relying on the mangerial foresight of the client was overlooked. There were instances where I believe that I should have pushed back and held my ground, so that I could remain invested and challenged. I learned that one should really truly believe in the project till the end, and that being driven by one's own satisfaction is more important than the client's satisfaction when it comes to attaining perfection.
